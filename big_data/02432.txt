Let us now turn to the case where the uid (or a mixture of uids) is conned within a slit-like pore.
In order to commence a simulation of the pressure-driven ow, it is required to provide a set of rules
deningthebehaviouroftheuidparticlesatwalls,andaprescriptionfortheconstructionofthewalls.
These rules should recover the well known cases of hydrodynamic ow such as the Poiseuille ow (i.e.,
a ow of a Newtonian uid with no-slip boundary conditions and a parabolic velocity prole). On the
other hand, for the ow of a polymeric uid (i.e., a non-Newtonian uid) the set of rules should lead to
the slip boundary conditions. The simplest set of rules comprise elastic reections off the wall [44, 45].
Unfortunately, they give rise to a hydrodynamic slip for Newtonian uids, as well as suffer from near-
wall density artifacts at higher density. This can be traced back to the fact that the atoms repelled each
otherstronglybutdidnotinteractwiththewalluntiltheyattemptedtocross[46].
Anumberofmoresophisticatedsetofruleshavebeensuggested.Oneoptionistoformthecrystalline
walls of a few layers of frozen (or having large mass) particles [47–53]. The interaction between the
bulk uid particles and those of the wall creates the near-wall drag which leads to the formation of the
Poiseuille ow. A drawback of this approach is the propagation of the crystalline order into the near-
wallregionsofbulkuid.Thiseffectisperfectlyphysicalfortheatomicmoleculardynamicssimulation,
where the solid wall mimics a real crystalline structure. However, for the mesoscopic DPD simulations,
each soft bead is assumed to represent a meso-scale portion of the material, on which scale the atomic
crystallinestructureissmeared-out.
This drawback can be avoided by using the structureless uid-like walls [46]. The walls in this case
are made of the uid conned in the slabs adjacent to the pore boundary, and the elastic reections
are applied on both sides of the boundary. Therefore, bulk and wall uid particles are immiscible. Still,
the interaction between near-wall beads on the opposite sides of the boundary creates a near-wall drag
ensuringno-slipboundaryconditionforNewtonianuids.
Anotherimportantissueinowsimulationistoavoidthesystemoverheatingduetothepresenceof
thebodyforce.Thisproblemwasaddressedinseveralstudies,cf.forexamplereferences[54,55].Inthe
molecular dynamics simulation, the excessive energy is absorbed by an external thermostat, in either
bulk or near-wall form [54]. In DPD simulations, the thermostat is “internal”, provided by the balance
between interparticle friction and random forces. For the case of a ow, some means for dissipation of
additional energy related to the body force should be provided. One of the elegant ways to do this is the
concept of a reverse ow [56–58]. In this approach, the simulation box contains two sub-ows driven
oppositely. The total force applied to the system is equal to zero and a no-slip boundary is formed at the
interfacebetweentwooppositeowsofNewtonianuids.
In our study, we combine both concepts by employing the uid-like walls on both boundaries of
a pore and initiating contraows (reverse ows) within them. Separation between the main pore and
contraow-containing walls prevents intermixing between the beads from both regions. This is impor-
tant both in the case when the ow of a mixture is considered, or in the case of polymer modied walls,
wherepolymerchainsaretetheredtotheboundarybetweenthemainporeanduid-likewall.However,
the existence of the reective boundaries does not prevent a friction between the beads located on the
opposite sides of the boundary, enabling the formation of the no-slip boundary condition for Newtonian
uids.

4. Conclusions
In this paper, we developed the simulation approach, which allows one to simulate the pressure-
driven ow in the pore with at and polymer-modied walls. It combines the earlier ideas of uid-like
walls and reverse ow. The former enables to avoid highly structured solid walls that usually lead to
near-wall artefacts. The latter introduces friction between oppositely owing streams which makes it
possible to conserve the total momenta and keep the temperature constant. Our system geometry con-
tainsthecentralmainpore“enveloped”bytwouid-likeporesoneachside.Theowforceisintroduced
in the main pore and oppositely directed contra-ow of the same magnitude — in both uid-like walls.
Simulation of the oligomer ow through the pore with at walls is used as a check for the credibility
of the method and reproduction of the hydrodynamic boundary conditions. Good thermostatting of the
system is achieved when the ow force magnitude does not exceed a certain threshold. For the case of
the oligomer length Lo > 4, we found the molecules adjacent to the central pore boundaries essentially
stretchedandalignedalongtheow,whereastheirshapeismoresphericalandlessalignedinthemiddle
ofapore.Thisprovidesthebasisforvariationofthelocalfrictionacrosstheporeand,asaconsequence,
thenon-parabolicityofthevelocityprolefortheoligomeruidandtheslipboundarycondition.
Thecaseofpolymer-modiedwallsisalsoconsideredwhenthepolymerbrushhastheformofstripes
arrangedperpendicularlytotheowdirection.Inthiscase,atacertainthresholdvalueoftheowforce,
one observes the pillar-to-lamellar transition induced by the ow which leads to the brush enveloping
the pore wall with a relatively smooth layer. At higher ow rates, the ow of oligomer is similar to the
case of at walls, although for the narrower effective pore size. The latter is estimated both from the
intersectionofdensityprolesforthebrushandtheowingoligomerandfromtheintegralequationfor
the average brush thickness. The effect of local stretching and alignment of oligomers near the walls of
theeffectiveporeisdetectedthesameasforthecaseofatwalls.
The method can be extended to more complex systems, namely: the ow of mixtures and their ow-
induced separation; the ow of amphiphilic molecules; the ow of complex macromolecules or their
solutions. Combined with the ne-tunable structure of the brush, this opens up a possibility to study
variousproblemsoftransportofoligo-andmacromoleculesthroughacomplexstructuredenvironment.
